Where did polio come from? Why epidemics appeared only in the late 1800s

A genuine historical puzzle. The poliovirus is ancient — an Egyptian carving from ~1400 BC shows a withered, polio-like leg — yet epidemic paralytic polio is strikingly modern. The first U.S. outbreak was Vermont, 1894 (~132 cases); the first big urban epidemic struck New York in 1916 (~27,000 cases). Before ~1890 polio existed only as rare, scattered "infantile paralysis." Strangely, the epidemics grew worse as sanitation, clean water, and hygiene improved — the opposite of typhoid, cholera, diphtheria, and TB, which were falling. Scientists still don't fully agree on why. The two main explanations:

1) The leading (mainstream) view — the "hygiene hypothesis." When water and food were routinely contaminated, nearly every baby caught poliovirus in its first months of life, while still protected by the mother's antibodies. That early, antibody-shielded infection was usually silent and gave lifelong immunity — paralysis was rare. Modern sanitation delayed first exposure to older childhood or adulthood, after maternal antibodies had faded, and polio is far more likely to paralyze when it first strikes an older, unprotected person. So cleaner living paradoxically turned a mild, near-universal infant infection into an epidemic crippler — a "disease of development," not of filth. This fits the timing and the age-shift well, though the exact drivers (why certain years and places, the role of more virulent strains) aren't fully settled.

2) The skeptic alternative — the pesticide / DDT theory. A minority view — voiced by physician Morton Biskind in the 1950s and revived in Forrest Maready's 2018 book The Moth in the Iron Lung and by some vaccine skeptics — argues the paralysis epidemics were caused or worsened by neurotoxic pesticides (lead arsenate, then DDT), citing a rough correlation between pesticide use and epidemic peaks, and crediting the decline to falling pesticide use rather than the vaccine.

Why mainstream scientists reject pesticides as the cause: the poliovirus was isolated and shown to cause the disease (grown in culture by Enders, Weller & Robbins in 1949 — Nobel 1954), it is contagious, and outbreaks spread like an infection, not a poisoning. Epidemics began in the 1890s, before DDT's insecticidal use (1939+); the decline tracks the 1955 Salk vaccine closely — years before DDT was restricted (1972) — and polio has since been eliminated in many countries by vaccination with no change in pesticide use. Pesticide nerve damage is real but is a different mechanism from viral paralysis.

The kernel worth keeping: there genuinely was a post-1955 diagnostic reclassification (the paralytic-case definition tightened and aseptic meningitis / other paralyses were split out) — that's real and is exactly what the next card below quantifies — and individual co-factors (heavy exertion, tonsillectomy) could worsen a given case. But "polio was just pesticide poisoning" doesn't survive the virus evidence. Best-supported answer: the hygiene / maternal-antibody hypothesis.

Polio: isolating the effect of the 1950s case-definition change

The definition change and the Salk vaccine both arrived in 1955, so they overlap on the main chart. To separate them we use a definition-immune anchor: deaths (which cannot be reclassified). Normalized to 1952 = 100, deaths fall as fast as — or faster than — paralytic cases, and the case-fatality rate does not jump. That means the definition change is real but accounts for only a minority of the decline; the bulk is genuine. One caveat: deaths aren't treatment-immune — positive-pressure ventilation / the first ICUs (1952) cut the fatality rate, so the death decline partly reflects better care and slightly overstates the fall in infections. (Even so, this cannot by itself split the real decline between vaccine and other factors at the 1955 inflection — see the README write-up.)

Hib: the mortality signal (via childhood meningitis)

Hib-specific deaths aren't recorded in standard U.S. mortality tables — Hib deaths were coded under meningitis and septicemia, not broken out. Since Hib was the leading cause of bacterial meningitis in young children before the vaccine, the childhood meningitis death rate is the best available proxy. The 1–4 year rate fell from ~1.0 per 100,000 (1987) to ~0.2 after the 1990 infant Hib vaccine. (The under-1 decline began earlier, largely from better meningitis treatment, then dropped further post-1990.) Source: NCHS Table HIST001R (1979–1998); this is all-cause meningitis, so it overstates Hib's share somewhat. The main chart's "Deaths per 100,000" view above now plots this same measured 1–4-year series, so the death view reflects real mortality instead of a back-calculated estimate.

⚠ DTP and all-cause mortality — an unresolved "non-specific effects" concern

Beyond protecting against their target diseases, vaccines may have "non-specific effects" on overall mortality. Observational studies from the Bandim Health Project (Guinea-Bissau) report that live vaccines (BCG, measles) are associated with lower all-cause child mortality, while inactivated whole-cell DTP has been associated with higher all-cause mortality — especially in girls — in high-mortality, low-income settings. The most-cited study (Mogensen, Aaby et al., EBioMedicine 2017; a 1980s natural experiment) found DTP-vaccinated infants had roughly 5× higher all-cause mortality than unvaccinated infants despite fewer target-disease cases; the authors wrote DTP "may kill more children from other causes than it saves."

A WHO/SAGE-commissioned systematic review (Higgins et al. 2016) likewise found most DTP studies pointed toward higher mortality — but rated the evidence very low quality (high risk of bias, e.g. frailty/ selection bias) and called for randomized trials. The evidence is observational, largely from one research group, and unresolved; WHO reviewed it and maintained its DTP recommendation while supporting more research. It concerns whole-cell DTP in high-mortality settings and does not mean DTP fails against diphtheria/tetanus/pertussis (it doesn't).

Why it's here: this is the clearest real-world reason to track all-cause mortality, not only disease-specific deaths — a disease-specific metric would miss an off-target effect entirely. A genuine open question that warrants the trials that haven't been done.

Related: natural measles/mumps and later disease (non-specific effects)

A Japanese cohort (JACC; Kubota et al., Atherosclerosis 2015; 103,836 adults followed ~20 years) found that adults who had had both measles and mumps as children had lower cardiovascular mortality (men HR 0.80, women 0.83) — part of the broader, unsettled "non-specific effects" idea (see the DTaP tab). Caveats: observational; childhood infection was self-reported decades later (recall/misclassification bias); it's a pre-vaccine birth cohort where these infections were near-universal, so the never-infected comparison group is unusual; association, not causation. It concerns natural infection, not vaccination, and does not argue against vaccinating — acute measles carries real risks (encephalitis, death, and ~2–3 years of "immune amnesia" that raises mortality from other infections).

Meningococcal: most of the decline came before the vaccine

This is a case where the headline ("cases fell after the vaccine") is true but misleading on its own. U.S. meningococcal incidence was already falling for roughly a decade before routine adolescent MenACWY arrived in 2005 — from about 1.1 per 100,000 in the mid-1990s to 0.40 by 2006 — for reasons that are not fully understood (it has long cycled naturally). Tellingly, serogroup B, which MenACWY does not cover, declined over the same period too — so a large part of the overall drop is a pre-existing secular trend, not the vaccine.

Where the vaccine's effect is measurable is narrower and real: serogroups C, W, and Y (the ones MenACWY targets) fell about 89% in 11–15-year-olds and 77% in 16–22-year-olds from the pre-vaccine to the post-booster era — steeper declines, in exactly the vaccinated age groups, than the background trend alone predicts (CDC, Mbaeyi et al.).

Since 2021 disease is rebounding (driven by serogroup Y), now above pre-pandemic levels and unusually affecting adults 30–60, Black adults, and people with HIV. Case-fatality stays high at ~10–15%.

Childhood vaccination coverage over time

Availability ≠ coverage. A vaccine's licensure year is not when the population became protected — coverage rises over years, and gaps drive residual disease and resurgences (e.g., the 1989–91 measles resurgence struck unvaccinated preschoolers decades after the 1963 vaccine). Source: CDC Pink Book Appendix E coverage levels, 1962–2016 (the Overview tab shows all vaccines). Further historical context: coverage_milestones.md.
