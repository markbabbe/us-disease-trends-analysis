Polio: isolating the effect of the 1950s case-definition change

The definition change and the Salk vaccine both arrived in 1955, so they overlap on the main chart. To separate them we use a definition-immune anchor: deaths (which cannot be reclassified). Normalized to 1952 = 100, deaths fall as fast as — or faster than — paralytic cases, and the case-fatality rate does not jump. That means the definition change is real but accounts for only a minority of the decline; the bulk is genuine. One caveat: deaths aren't treatment-immune — positive-pressure ventilation / the first ICUs (1952) cut the fatality rate, so the death decline partly reflects better care and slightly overstates the fall in infections. (Even so, this cannot by itself split the real decline between vaccine and other factors at the 1955 inflection — see the README write-up.)

Early-century death rate per 100,000 (1900–1960) — before the measles vaccine

Approximate, from NCHS/Historical Statistics mortality-decline series via the partial Death Registration Area. Shows that case fatality fell sharply before vaccines (antibiotics, nutrition, supportive care). Both measles and pertussis death rates dropped ~95%+ over this window; the measles vaccine (1963) came after it.

Childhood vaccination coverage over time

Availability ≠ coverage. A vaccine's licensure year is not when the population became protected — coverage rises over years, and gaps drive residual disease and resurgences (e.g., the 1989–91 measles resurgence struck unvaccinated preschoolers ~26 years after the 1963 vaccine; note the dip in measles coverage through the 1980s). Hollow markers (pre-1994) are approximate historical anchors from published reports; filled diamonds are CDC NIS data (the survey began 1994). See coverage_milestones.md for sources and caveats.
